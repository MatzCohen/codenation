1.1 Describing the content and layout for each page


My website is made up of 10 pages in total, It has 3 main pages, A ‘Homepage’ (index page), an ‘About Me’ page and a ‘Contact Me’ page.
The fourth page is a ‘Projects’ page which is a gateway to a further 6 pages, one for each Project Outcome.
All pages have the same header, navigation bar and footer, each section in every page has a thick border to give the website the feel of consistency.
The header together with the navigation bar always remain fixed and for a nicer effect, the scrolled page can be seen through the slightly transparent header background.
The navigation bar has 4 links that are highlighted when moused over, the Home, About me & Projects links are on the left of the bar the Contact Me link is on the right on its own for better emphasis.
All pages and navigation bar are sizeable including their content.
The footer is in the same background colour as the header and has a secondary link to the ‘Contact Me’ page.
The main ‘Projects’ page has 2 sections, one for each unit and within the section 3 buttons that link to the 3 outcomes of that unit.
The ‘About Me’ page has 4 sections, the first with my picture and some info about me, the other 3 sections detail my connection to computers, past, present, and future.
Finally, the ‘Contact Me’ page another image of me, my name, phone number and clickable links for sending me an email or launching a new tab with my LinkedIn page.
This page also has an enquiry form

1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites


Copyright and other intellectual property laws affect websites because it may restrict what you are able to publish. The best way to get around this is first, get permission from the original author, second, if you want to publish information on your site from another site, link to it and perhaps get permission to publish an excerpt from the original site.

1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using webpages and how we can overcome them


People with disabilities often use assistive technologies to access websites. A website that is accessible is designed to function properly with assistive technologies so that the information and functionality that is fundamental to the website is made accessible to every visitor. In some cases, such as in the case of colour blindness, no assistive technologies are used. Instead, websites that are accessible to these visitors consider these issues as they design their website and make proper use of visual elements.

1.7 Describe which are the appropriate filetypes for websites


The 5 most common image formats for websites are JPEG, GIF, BMP, TIFF and PNG. The most popular out of the 5 is JPEG.
Other file formats that are used on websites are Mpeg, AVI and MP4 for video.
For text documents the most common formats are TXT, HTML and PDF

1.8 Storing and retrieving files effectively


The importance of record-keeping and filing systems cannot be too highly stressed. A well-planned system contributes significantly to efficiency of operation as well as to a company's image. Whether records are filed in a computer or in a steel cabinet, they have to be readily accessible.

  • Confidential information must be stored securely to prevent a breach of data protection
  • Electronic files must be password encrypted and if they are emailed you should send the password in a second separate email to avoid unauthorised access
  • Files under the data protection act state you should not hold information longer than necessary so organisations should have in place a set length of time the hold information before its destroyed
  • The data protection act states that information/data should not be kept for longer than required, most companies will keep data for 5-6 years before destroying it
  • Account information should be kept between 6-7 years
